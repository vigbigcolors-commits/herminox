Locked capital: the quiet cost of maybe I’ll keep it

Every undecided purchase is a mini inventory problem. When the window closes, the decision was made for you — usually badly.

Locked capital ≈ Σ price of items still inside return window and undecided

BehaviorCapital effectFix
Buy now, decide laterHigh lockDeadline scoreboard
Decide at purchaseLow lockStricter cart
Miss windowCapital becomes costReminders + fewer impulse SKUs

Worked example

Cart of maybes. Three items at $80, $120, $200 undecided = $400 locked. Missing one deadline on the $200 item costs more than hours of comparison shopping saved.

Indecision is an interest-free loan to the retailer

Your card is charged; the product sits; the window shrinks. Tracking locked capital makes the cost of delay visible — which is usually enough to decide.

Pair with cost-per-use for durables

If you are delaying because you cannot tell whether premium was worth it, run cost-per-use with conservative wears. Then keep or return before the deadline.

Checklist

  • List undecided items
  • Sum locked capital
  • Sort by deadline
  • Decide or schedule decision
  • Return labels started early for heavy items

FAQ

Is locked capital a real accounting term?

Here it is a practical cash concept: money spent on items you might still reverse. Useful for households and operators alike.

Should I return everything by default?

No. Return what fails the decision criteria. The point is a decision, not maximal returns.

What about restocking fees?

Include them in the keep-vs-return math when applicable — they reduce the value of returning late or opened items.
